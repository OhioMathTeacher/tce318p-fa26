Week 9 · Tuesday — Studio

Act II · design the Before · how do you warm up the thinking without giving the task away?
Today's goals: Design the Before of your lesson — a short routine that opens on the same mathematics your task investigates and surfaces your learner's entry thinking. First we judge real routines against a shared task — does each warm up the thinking, or give the task away? Then you build two candidate Befores — as slides — for your own rich task.

1 · Before, or give-away? — judging routines (~25 min)

  • Your rich task is the During — the main event. The Before is a brief, low-floor warm-up on the same concept that surfaces how your learner already thinks. The danger is the pull to help: a routine that hands over the strategy, picture, or procedure the task is meant to reveal gives the task away.
  • Together we walk four real routines against one shared task. For each: does it warm up the thinking, or hand the task away? Argue it — some clearly prime, some clearly give it away, some are worth a fight.
  • We land one principle: a good Before warms up the same mathematics and surfaces entry thinking, but withholds the task's move — so the task can still reveal what the child brings.

Purpose: you already sense when a warm-up "gives it away." Today you name what makes the difference. You ran these routines in Week 3 as a participant; now you judge them as a diagnostician.

2 · Your turn — build two Befores for your task (~35 min)

  • Consider (~10 min). Open the routines library and go to the shelf for your concept — number talks, Which One Doesn't Belong?, Same & Different, Notice & Wonder, estimation, and more. Find two routines that could warm up your rich task without giving it away.
  • Go around (~5 min). Each of us names the routine we're leaning toward — and why it fits our task.
  • Develop (~20 min). Build two candidate Befores as slides — 1–2 slides each (Google Slides, PowerPoint, or PDF), with how you'd pose each — and run each past the test: could my learner still show me something of their own on the task, or did this warm-up already do its work? Making a visual routine (a shape, a Which One Doesn't Belong? image)? nVoke — the Week 3 shape tool — renders crisp figures you can screenshot into a slide.

Purpose: your task is the filter. A routine earns its place as your Before only if it primes the thinking your task investigates — and leaves the task's move for the child to make.

3 · Share out — Before, or give-away? (~15 min)

  • Several of us put a candidate Before on the table — pose it the way you'd run it with your learner, and name the one move your task depends on that it must not hand over.
  • The room judges it the way we judged the four routines this morning: does it warm up the thinking, or give the task away? Push on the close calls.

Purpose: you turned the diagnostic lens on four real routines this morning; now the class turns it on your design. You leave with a sharper read on your own pairing — and other people's tasks stretch how you see your own.

Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • Before Thursday, finish your two slide-based Befores (with how you'd pose each), and a sentence on the one move your task depends on — the thing your routine must not hand over.
  • Thursday is a working session — classmates will consider your task, then do both of your routines; you'll get feedback on how each aligns with your task and whether either gives the move away, then pressure-test the stronger one with a genAI partner. That routine is the Before of the lesson you're assembling next week (see the Math Routine assignment).
