Week 9 · Thursday — Studio

Act II · test the Before · does it warm up the thinking without doing the task's work? · two routines meet real responders
Today's goals: You brought two slide-based routines. Today classmates consider your task, then do each routine — and you find out which one warms up students' entry thinking while leaving the task's move for students to make. Then a genAI partner audits the stronger one for over-scaffolding. Human first, machine second.

1 · What your Before has to do (~8 min)

  • The routine opens the lesson — the Before. Its job is to surface how students already think about your concept, so the warm-up itself becomes a first slice of evidence.
  • So today we test two things: does the routine warm up the same mathematics your task investigates, and does it hold back the move the task is meant to reveal — or quietly hand it over?

2 · Share your task, name the move (~7 min)

  • With your Field Prep Partner(s) — the group you're matched with — share your rich task so they understand what it's for.
  • Name the one move your task depends on — the strategy, representation, or procedure it exists to make visible (placing a fraction on a line, decomposing by place value, regrouping…). That's what they'll watch each routine for.

Purpose: unlike last week, they see the task first — you can't tell whether a warm-up gives the move away without knowing the move. Naming it out loud is the whole test.

3 · They do both routines — you watch (~35 min)

  • Run each of your two routines with your partner(s) — pose it as you would with students, ask "how do you know?", and let them actually work it. Don't steer.
  • Watch for three things as they respond: is the routine well-posed (clear to run, or confusing)? Does it surface entry thinking — even thinking that surprises you, that goes somewhere you didn't anticipate? And does it hand over the move your task is meant to reveal?
  • Then, together: which of your two routines aligns better with your task, and which stays clear of the give-away? Capture their read — and take turns, so everyone runs theirs.

Purpose: a routine is only a diagnostic if thinking surfaces on its own. Actually running it — not just judging it on paper — is where you learn it's confusing, or that a real responder goes somewhere you never planned for.

4 · Then Alkimi — the over-scaffold audit (~20 min + work time)

  • Take your stronger routine — with your task and the move you're withholding — to Alkimi, using the prompt in the Math Routine assignment. Ask it to hold the routine beside the task: does it give away the move? where does it prime, and where does it give away? Follow up.
  • Alkimi is a critic, not a designer — it may catch a give-away your partners missed, but the routine stays yours. If it hands you a routine, push back.
  • Start the Math Routine assignment: choose and refine one routine from both reads, and begin the two-reads reflection. Read the assignment together first — ask your questions now.

Purpose: the human run shows you how the routine behaves; Alkimi's audit is the structural check on the one thing novices miss — whether the Before quietly does the task's work.

Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • Finish the Math Routine assignment for this week — carry both reads (your partners running your routines, then Alkimi's over-scaffold audit) into the routine you choose, and record what you changed and what you rejected.
  • Next week you assemble the whole nest — Before, During, and After — into one lesson to investigate your concept.
