Week 1 · Tuesday — Studio

Mathematics PK–5 · Learner · figuring math out, not being told it
Today's goals: Figure out the rules of unfamiliar puzzles by reasoning from a worked example — and notice that in mathematics you can figure things out; you don't wait to be told the method.

1 · Getting started (5 min)

  • 📄 Today's worksheet: Three Reasoning Puzzles — one solved example per puzzle; infer the rules, then solve the fresh one.
  • Sit with a partner.
  • Today there are no instructions handed to you. That's the point.
  • For each puzzle: review the solved example to understand how it works → work together to solve a fresh one → come back together for a 5-minute whole-group debrief. We rotate the puzzle, not the seats.
  • 📓 Keep paper handy for your own notes. Later this week you'll read the McFeetors article and write a short journal entry about one moment today when your own puzzling turned into real mathematical reasoning. You won't remember the details later, so write them down now — what you were stuck on, and what helped you get unstuck.

2 · Puzzle 1 — Calcudoku (~20 min)

  • You get a solved example — no rules. With your partner, figure out the goal and the rules before you solve a fresh one.

Purpose: experience inductive reasoning — inferring rules from examples — and productive struggle.

3 · Puzzle 2 — Sumplete (~20 min)

  • Same idea: infer the rule from a solved board, then solve a new one. (Delete the right cells so every row and column hits its target sum.)

Purpose: a different operations structure — the same "reason from the example" habit.

4 · Puzzle 3 — Arithmagons (~20 min)

  • Figure out how the numbers on the edges relate to the numbers in the corners — then work it both directions.

Purpose: inverse reasoning — inferring which direction the operation runs.

If you finish early

  • Our games were designed as one-person games. How might we change the rules to make these games multi-player? Are some better-suited for that than others? Why?
  • Is there more than one solution to the puzzles? Is it possible for a player to know if they've found all of the solutions? How do you know?
Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • How did you figure out each puzzle's rules? Where did you get stuck — and what got you unstuck?
  • Before Thursday: read McFeetors & Palfy (2017) (link in Canvas → JSTOR), then start your reading-homework journal entry — rank the opening moves and defend your ranking (how the journal works: Reading Journal).
  • Thursday: we'll make your own thinking visible with the app — play, then replay and annotate your own solve.
