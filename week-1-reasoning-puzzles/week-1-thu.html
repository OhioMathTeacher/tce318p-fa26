Week 1 · Thursday — Clinic

Mathematics PK–5 · Learner · making your own thinking visible
Today's goals: Name what Tuesday was — inductive reasoning, where the struggle was the point and process mattered more than the answer — and then do what commercial math apps never let you do: replay and annotate your own solve.

1 · Debrief + reading (~30 min)

  • Back to Tuesday's puzzles: how did you figure out the rules? Where was the struggle?
  • In-class skim → think-pair-share of McFeetors & Palfy (2017).
  • Together, name what Tuesday was: inductive reasoning; the struggle was the point; process over answer.

Purpose: put language to the disposition you just lived — reasoning, not being told.

2 · Exploration Starter — watch your own thinking (~25 min)

  • Play a Metadoku board, then play it back and annotate your own solve — "you did have a strategy; name it."
  • Feature jigsaw: your lane surfaces one part of the app for the room — playback-scrub · annotate · hint/undo-as-data · find-all · the report.
  • Everyone downloads the report and submits it — this is your Thinking-Analysis Report (see the assignment page in Canvas).

Purpose: the first stop in watching thinking is your own (own → classmate → field).

3 · Question closer (QFT) (~20 min · in pairs)

Your first Question Formulation: instead of answering questions, you practice asking them. Four rules — ask as many as you can · don't stop to answer or judge · write each exactly as stated · turn any statement into a question.

  • Q-focus: "You can't do math you haven't been taught."
  • Brainstorm 10+ questions off that statement (follow the four rules — don't answer them).
  • Sort each question as open or closed; flip one of each and notice what changes.
  • Prioritize your top 3, then deposit one into our class question bank.

Turn a provocation into questions worth exploring — the engine of the course. Not collected — this is thinking practice; your one question joins the class bank (the seed for the inquiry work ahead).

If you finish early

  • Compare two of your own playbacks — did your strategy change from one board to the next? What changed it?
Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • You just did as a Learner what you'll one day ask children to do. Hold onto that.
  • After class: read Martinez (2006), "What Is Metacognition?" — short, and pointed right at the playback you just did (link in Canvas → JSTOR).
  • Take-home: Solution Report #1 on one puzzle (see Canvas).
