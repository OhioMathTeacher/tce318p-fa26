About this course

TCE 318P is a methods course for preservice PK–5 teachers, focused on teaching mathematics for understanding. You will design, enact, and revise lessons that are responsive to students' developmental needs, mathematical thinking, and lived experiences — learning to read curriculum materials, assess student work, and reflect on practice through field-based experience.

The arc of the semester. The whole course builds toward one act of evidence-based inquiry: you develop a question about how students understand a mathematical concept, build a lesson to investigate it, take it into a real classroom, and report honestly on what you found.
  • Act I — Foundations (Wks 1–5). The core moves: reasoning, listening and questioning, geometry and van Hiele, differentiation, and assessment.
  • Act II — Build the investigation (Wks 6–10). A concept, a research question, and a nested Before–During–After lesson designed to reveal student thinking.
  • Act III — Field & report (Wks 11–15). Enact the lesson, collect student work, and communicate what you learned in a final report.

Course objectives

By the end of this course, candidates will be able to:

  • Design and adapt lesson plans that align with standards, address developmental progressions, and respond to student thinking.
  • Implement effective teaching practices, including questioning, active listening, and facilitation of rich mathematical tasks.
  • Analyze and assess student work to inform instructional decisions.
  • Use observation and field experience to plan targeted instruction.
  • Integrate principles of access, inclusion, and differentiation into mathematics instruction.
  • Engage in reflective practice to improve teaching and communicate professional insights.

Working with AI & Gemini

Using generative AI is your choice, not a requirement. When you do use it, use it as a thinking partner, not an answer generator — the reasoning has to be yours.

  • We recommend Gemini, since you all have Miami Pro accounts. Any tool is fine; none is required.
  • Human first. A person reads your work before a machine does. We practice this all semester — you test your own thinking, and a colleague reads your draft, before an AI ever weighs in.
  • Disclose it. Say how you used AI in your work (a sentence is enough), and never present AI-invented facts or citations as real.
  • Todd-in-a-Can is our course AI reviewer, built for the final paper. Run it as often as it helps; a human peer reads your draft too.

Policies

Academic integrity

Your work should reflect honesty and respect for learning. Cheating, plagiarism, fabrication, unauthorized collaboration, or misrepresentation are prohibited and handled under Miami University's Academic Integrity Policy. Cite all sources you use, including AI assistance.

Privacy (FERPA)

Everything on this public site is de-identified — no student names, work, or grades. In your own assignments, use pseudonyms and remove identifying details from any student work — in text, image, audio, or video — before it goes in.

Professional dispositions

As a candidate for teaching licensure, you are expected to meet professional standards of practice: demonstrating commitment and responsibility, building respectful professional relationships, and engaging in critical, reflective practice.

Attendance

This is a Block course; participation in each element of the block is contingent on the others. Missing more than one seminar or more than three field-placement days — made up or not — can result in a Statement of Concern and may result in being dropped from all courses in the block.

Three required Block 2 seminars meet via Zoom (Field Placement Expectations · Block 3 overview · Student Teaching). Dates and the join link will be announced in class.

Course tools

Small, self-contained apps we use through the semester. Most run in any browser and record your work so you can replay your own reasoning.

  • Journaler — our reading-and-writing app: read closely, make margin notes, and keep your dated weekly Reading Journal (Act I).
  • Research Question Studio — shape your research question from your concept and student work (Week 7).
  • Metacognition suite — Metadoku, Metaplete, Metagons: puzzles that record your solving so you can replay your own reasoning.
  • Pentomino Builder — build differentiated pentomino tasks tagged by Depth of Knowledge (Week 4).
  • nVoke — flash-and-respond "what do you see?" (Week 3).
