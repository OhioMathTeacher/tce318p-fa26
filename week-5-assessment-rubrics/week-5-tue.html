Week 5 · Tuesday — Studio

Assessment · rubrics · Assessor · one work sample, many rubrics
Today's goals: Explore rubrics as an assessor's lens. Score the same real student work several ways, watch the score move, and name what each rubric actually measures — the lens you'll one day point at a single real learner.

1 · Score it cold (~10 min)

  • On the handout: the NAEP "Amusement Park" task, four real student work samples (each got the numbers right), and the rubrics.
  • Score one sample 0–4, no rubric, just your gut. Put your number on the board.

Purpose: with no shared lens, our scores scatter — and a gut score hides what we were actually rewarding.

2 · Same samples, different rubrics — jigsaw (~30 min)

  • Your table is assigned one rubric — not all four. Score all four samples with it, and record each score and the reason it earned that score. Then we pool every table's scores into one grid.
Rubric 1 (NAEP scoring guide) the item's own guide — Extended · Satisfactory · Partial · Minimal · Incorrect.
Rubric 2 (Temple ISD) the "did they get it right?" lens — correctness and clarity.
Rubric 3 (Charles–Lester–O'Daffer) three separate scores — Understanding · Planning · Getting an answer.
Rubric 4 (Maryland) one thing only — is the mathematical communication clear?
  • Watch one sample in particular — its labels are backwards: correct procedure, but 12 games and 24 shows (the numbers are swapped). Does your rubric reward the thinking, or punish the answer?

Purpose: the rubric is the lens. Swap the lens and the "same" work looks different.

3 · Why did the score move? (~25 min)

  • Line up the pooled grid and take one sample across the row: the same child is Satisfactory under one rubric, low under another, high under a third. Why?
  • Name each rubric by its type — we'll put the words up together: holistic (one overall score) vs analytic (a score per dimension); specific (built for this task) vs general (fits many tasks). You'll read the full framework for homework (McGatha & Darcy).
  • The weighting tells you what a rubric values: Rubric 3 gives Understanding 6 points but Getting an answer only 3 — it says reasoning matters more than the answer. Rubric 2 and Rubric 4 make the opposite bet.

Purpose: a rubric makes some things visible and others invisible — there is no view from nowhere. Choosing a rubric is choosing what counts.

4 · Which lens shows the learner? (~15 min)

  • McGatha's point: assessment can be for the student (formative), not just to them (summative). A bare number tells a child little; the right lens tells you where to teach next.
  • If your goal is to see where a learner is — not just grade them — which rubric would you reach for, and why?
  • That choice is the Assessor's move. Thursday you'll aim this lens at one real child — the field study begins.
Wrap-up & looking ahead:

  • Thursday: the field study, in one picture — how the whole back half of the course works, and how today's lens becomes the way you profile one real learner.
