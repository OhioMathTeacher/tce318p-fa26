Week 4 · Tuesday — Studio

Measurement & Data · area · Planner · a question for every learner
Today's goals: Explore differentiation through a study of pentominoes. Discover the pentominoes, solve a range of problems and sort them by depth (DOK), then start reshaping your own questions into tasks for learners.

1 · Discover the pentominoes (~20 min)

  • With a partner, build pentominoes from snap cubes and sketch each on Activity Sheet 1 (Making Pieces). A pentomino is five squares joined edge to edge; flips and turns of the same shape count as one.
  • How do you know when you have them all? Build a system, not a pile.

Purpose: the "how do you know you're done?" struggle is the Learner move again — building a system to be sure you've found them all.

2 · Problem solving with pentominoes (~20 min)

Consider these five questions about pentominoes:

  1. Find the area of each pentomino. What do you notice?
  2. How many distinct pentomino shapes are there? How do you know?
  3. Sort the pentominoes by perimeter.
  4. Make a larger version of a pentomino using any four pieces.
  5. Design a frame that only the L, I, M, and T shapes can fill.

Which, if any, seem easier? Which ask for more advanced understanding?

  • The levels of complexity are often described using Webb's Depth of Knowledge (DOK 1–4). It doesn't track harder numbers; it tracks how deep the thinking goes: recall a fact (1) → apply a skill (2) → reason and justify (3) → design and investigate (4).

Purpose: sorting the questions surfaces the hidden dimension — not how hard the numbers are, but how deep the thinking goes. DOK is the depth dial you'll design with in the Builder.

3 · Double your table's pentomino (~20 min)

  • Each table gets a different pentomino — T L Z I P M. On Activity Sheet 2 (Enlarged Pentominoes), double yours using four distinct pentominoes.
  • Can you do it? If not, why not?
  • As you work, jot down the questions that come up for you — whatever you find yourself wondering about the shapes, the doubling, or the pieces.

Purpose: the doubling is genuine problem-solving — and the questions you catch as they arise are the raw material for the next step.

4 · Come back together (~20 min)

  • Debrief. Could you double your pentomino with four distinct pieces? Which shapes couldn't — and why not? Put the questions you jotted up on the board.
  • Turn teacher — together. As a class, rewrite those questions into ones you'd pose to grade 2–4 students studying pentominoes.
  • Then sort. Place the kid-questions into DOK piles — recall (1), apply (2), reason (3), design (4). Where do they land? Which piles are empty?
  • Name it. Turning your own questions into questions for kids, pitched across a range of depths, is differentiation — a question for every learner. Two dials: depth (DOK) and size (which shape you hand which learner).

If you finish early

  • Two pentominoes can't be doubled with four distinct pieces. Find them — and convince us why not.
