Week 2 · Tuesday — Studio

Number & Base-Ten PK–5 · Listener → Questioner · listening to thinking out loud
Today's goals: Make your reasoning public, and practice posing questions that open a peer's thinking rather than close it — without ever telling. Today you listen out loud; Thursday you'll do it on paper.

1 · Debrief the reading + set the stance (~10 min)

  • You read Reinhart before today. Quick debrief: what makes a question open vs. closed? Where does the urge to tell take over?
  • Name the two roles for today: the Doer works the problem out loud; the Thinker asks questions that draw out the Doer's reasoning and never tells.

Purpose: put the reading to work right away — set the ask-don't-tell stance before you pose.

2 · Solve in two groups (~25 min)

  • Two rich Number & Base-Ten tasks, one per group — each group becomes the expert on its task.
  • Blue Squares on the Hundred Chart
  • Green Greatest Product
  • Work from your handout (Blue group: Blue only · Green group: Green only). Solve it so someone else could follow your thinking.

Purpose: both tasks were chosen because they can be seen many ways — the raw material a good question can open up.

3 · Pose both ways (~40 min · 20 each)

  • Blue poses Squares on the Hundred Chart to Green and facilitates — asking, not telling. Then Green poses Greatest Product to Blue.
  • Everyone meets both tasks and gets reps facilitating. Rotate the active questioner so it isn't just one or two voices.
  • Watch for the moment you want to tell — and ask a question instead.

Purpose: enact the questioner's move — you've read never-tell; now catch yourself when the urge to tell shows up. Thursday we name it and turn listening onto paper.

If you finish early

  • Start the Week 2 Reading Homework — the reading for Thursday.
Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • One-line gut-check: how did it feel to ask instead of tell?
  • Homework (before Thursday): complete the Week 2 Reading Homework.
  • Thursday: name the questioning you just did, work the hundred chart together, then read a child's place-value thinking on paper.
