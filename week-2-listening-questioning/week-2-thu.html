Week 2 · Thursday — Clinic

Number & Base-Ten PK–5 · Listener → Questioner · listening to thinking on paper
Today's goals: Put language to the discourse you lived Tuesday — open vs. closed, funnel vs. focus, never-tell — then turn that same listening onto a child's written work: diagnose what one student understands, and craft questions that open their thinking.

1 · Debrief + name the discourse (~30 min)

  • Compare Tuesday's Blue/Green solutions: how did you see it? who saw it differently?
  • Watch a couple of short anti-model clips (a teacher doing all the talking) and one elicit-the-thinking clip, and skim Reinhart (2000).
  • Together, code the moves you just made while posing: open / closed questions, funneling / focusing.

Purpose: name what you already did — the questioner's moves become visible and repeatable.

2 · Making reasoning visible — one child, two ways (~30 min)

  • Listening moves from out loud to on paper: you get one third grader's actual work on 1003 − 999, solved two ways — the standard algorithm (which breaks down across the zeros) and a compensation shortcut (which lands cleanly). The two answers disagree.
  • Diagnose the solution: what does this child seem to understand — or not — about the mathematics? Justify with details from the work.
  • Then brainstorm 5 open, non-leading questions you might ask this child to build a stronger understanding — with a brief reason each is a good question.
  • This is your MQR — an in-class, no-AI diagnosis (see the assignment page in Canvas).

Purpose: on paper you can't probe — you must infer. Reading a child before you respond to one is the Listener's core work.

3 · Question closer (QFT) (~20 min · in pairs)

Your weekly Question Formulation — four rules: ask as many as you can · don't stop to answer or judge · write each exactly as stated · turn any statement into a question.

  • Q-focus: "A teacher should never tell students what they can figure out for themselves." (Reinhart)
  • Brainstorm 10+ questions off that statement (follow the four rules — don't answer them).
  • Sort each as open or closed — you've studied this move all week — and flip one of each.
  • Prioritize your top 3, then deposit one into our class question bank.

Turn a provocation into questions worth exploring. Not collected — thinking practice; your one question joins the class bank that seeds the inquiry work ahead.

If you finish early

  • Of your 5 questions for the child, which one would you open with — and what makes it the strongest first move?
Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • You listened to thinking in both channels this week — out loud and on paper. Same act; different evidence.
  • After class: read Russell (2000), "Developing Computational Fluency with Whole Numbers" — why a flexible repertoire (like the compensation move you just diagnosed) is the real goal, not one procedure (link in Canvas → JSTOR).
  • Take-home: Solution Report #2 — Blue and Green, ≥ 2 ways each (see Canvas).
  • Next week: step from Listener to Poser — you'll build the tasks worth asking about (geometry).
