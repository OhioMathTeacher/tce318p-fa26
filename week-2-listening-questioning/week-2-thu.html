Week 2 · Thursday — Clinic

Number & Base-Ten PK–5 · Listener → Questioner · listening to thinking on paper
Today's goals: Turn your listening onto paper. After comparing Tuesday's solutions and the hundred-chart reading, work the hundred chart as a questioning task — then read one child's written place-value thinking, diagnose it, and ask questions that open it up.

1 · Debrief the tasks + the reading (~20 min)

  • Compare Tuesday's Blue and Green solutions: how did you see it? who saw it differently?
  • Debrief the Hundred Chart Challenge reading (Switzer): where does the teacher ask instead of tell?

Purpose: connect Tuesday's posing to the reading before you work the hundred chart yourself.

2 · Hundred Chart Challenge (~25 min)

  • Each table gets a different Challenge card — all on the same two numbers, so what changes is the question, not the numbers. Work your card together: how would you solve it on the hundred chart?
  • Each table brings its thinking to the fishbowl in turn. As they share, the rest of the class asks open questions to draw out their reasoning — never tell.
  • We record the fishbowl — you'll analyze it for your reading journal.

Purpose: six add-and-subtract structures on the same numbers surface place value on the chart — and the whole class rehearses the never-tell questioning you read about.

3 · The MQR (~30 min)

  • Now on paper: read one child's written work and respond.
  • This is Pedagogical Excursion #2 — in class, no AI, collected at the end of class (see Canvas).

Purpose: on paper you can't probe — you must infer. Reading a child before you respond is the Listener's core work.

If you finish early

  • Of your questions for the child, which one would you open with — and why is it the strongest first move?
Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • You listened in both channels this week — out loud on Tuesday, on paper today. Same act; different evidence.
  • Reading Journal: write your dated entry for this week (see the assignment).
  • Next week: step from Listener to Poser — you'll build the tasks worth asking about (geometry).
  • Before next class (genAI): take a photo of your question & rationale sheet and ask genAI to comment on your rationale. Bring what it pushed you on — would you revise? Use this prompt:

I'm training to be a teacher. A third grader solved 83 − 18 as 8 − 3 = 5 and 8 − 1 = 7, getting 75 (subtracting the smaller digit from the larger in each column). The attached photo shows a question I would ask this student and my reasons for asking it. Please don't re-diagnose the student or rewrite my question — just react to my reasoning: is my rationale specific to this student's error, or too general? How could I make it more specific to this child's thinking?
