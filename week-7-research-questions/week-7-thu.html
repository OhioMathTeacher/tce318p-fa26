Week 7 · Thursday — Studio

Act II · aim at my question · the Question Formulation Technique · you write the questions; the machine only sharpens them
Today's goals: Turn the pile of wonderings from Tuesday into one research question you could actually investigate with your target learner — one your nested lesson (a routine, a rich task, and a discussion) could help you answer. You'll storm questions from the child's own work, sharpen them with a genAI partner, and land on one.

1 · How the Studio works (~10 min)

  • A short demo of the Research Question Studio, tab by tab, and time for your questions before you start.
  • Everything stays in your browser. The child's real name and raw work never leave your laptop — you'll redact the work in the app, and only a de-identified version is exported.

2 · Build your question in the Studio (~45 min)

  • Work through the five tabs at your own pace:
  1. Learner — the grade, the mathematics ahead, and the adaptation you're weighing.
  2. Collected work — add the samples you brought and redact them, so the evidence stays but the child stays anonymous.
  3. Brainstorm — using the four rules, storm as many questions about the child's thinking as you can, then mark each one open or closed.
  4. Check names — a scan to catch anything identifying before it leaves your machine.
  5. Hand off — produce one PDF: your write-up, the redacted work, and your question storm.

Purpose: looking hard at what the child actually did is what produces real questions. The rules push you past your first two or three; open/closed makes you feel which questions open thinking up.

3 · Sharpen with the Research Question Bot (~15 min)

  • Give your handoff PDF to the Research Question Bot. It will widen your questions and pressure-test them — is this investigable? is it about the child's reasoning, or just whether they got it right?
  • It will not hand you a question. If it tries, that's your cue to push back. You keep authorship; it only makes your own questions sharper.
  • As you weigh its suggestions, keep one test in front of you: could the nested lesson you build next — a routine, a rich task, and a discussion — actually help you answer this?

Purpose: the move you're learning is AI widens, my criteria narrow. The human noticing is the source of the question; the machine is a way to see more angles on it.

4 · Land one question (~10 min)

  • Prioritize down to the single research question you'll carry into the field, and write one or two sentences on why you chose it.
  • Read it against the test: is it open, is it investigable, and could your nested lesson gather evidence on it? Adjust if not.

If you finish early

  • Trade questions with a partner. Ask a question about their child's work that they're too close to see — then let them do the same for you. Both of you keep your own question.
Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • This week's deliverable is the Research Question — the question you land today, plus the record of how you sharpened it (the assignment has the details).
  • Next week you design the first piece of that lesson — a rich task for this learner, aimed squarely at the question you just wrote.
