Week 8 · Tuesday — Studio

Act II · sharpen the question · what makes a research question stronger? · you can spot a good one — can you make yours better?
Today's goals: Turn your in-progress research question into one strong enough to steer a task. You'll uncover the moves that make a question more researchable, sharpen each other's questions in a gallery walk, then find the NRICH shelf that matches your learner's mathematics.

1 · Why sharpen it again? (~5 min)

  • The question you landed last week is the spine of the whole back half of the course. Before it goes hunting for a task, we harden it — with a week's distance and fresh eyes.
  • Read yours once more: is it still open? Is it about how the child thinks, or has it drifted toward whether they got it right?

2 · What makes a question stronger? (~25 min)

  • We look together at pairs of research questions — a first draft, then a sharpened version of the same question. For each, before the reveal: what would make this stronger? Then we compare, and name the move.
  • We collect what we find into a shared set of moves — the handful of changes that turn a vague question into an investigable one.
  • Then you try them on the practice questions — first-draft questions with no revision. Which move would you run, and what's your stronger version? Work them together.

Purpose: you already know a strong question when you see one. Today's work is naming the moves — so you can make your own stronger, not just judge it. (And a strong question can still lead to an honest "the evidence was mixed" — sharper doesn't mean tidier.)

3 · Gallery walk — sharpen each other's questions (~25 min)

  • Post your in-progress question on chart paper. Walk the room in pairs, and on each poster write questions — not fixes — through three lenses:
  • What does this make you wonder about the child's thinking?
  • Is it open and about understanding, or does it slip toward "did they get it right"?
  • What evidence would answer it — could one task show it?
  • Back at your own poster: read what your colleagues asked, and revise your question.

Purpose: a colleague can see what you're too close to see. Writing questions — not answers — on someone's inquiry is the same move you're learning to make with children.

4 · Find your NRICH shelf (~10 min)

  • With your sharpened question, open NRICH and go to the collection for your grade and the mathematics you're investigating.
  • Bookmark one or two tasks that might make your learner's thinking visible. You'll test and build them Thursday — today, just find the shelf.

Purpose: your sharpened question is the filter. A task earns a closer look only if it could surface the understanding your question is about.

If you finish early

  • Trade questions with someone whose grade is far from yours, and run the moves on a question about mathematics you don't teach. Do the moves still work?
Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • Before Thursday, bring your revised research question and a rich task tied to it — one you might adapt from NRICH. Don't solve it yet; on Thursday people who've never seen it will. (If you have a research partner, find one task together.)
  • Thursday is a working session — your Field Prep Partner(s) solve your task cold, you compare it against how real children solved it, and only then do you pressure-test it with a genAI partner. That task is the first piece of the lesson you'll build over the next three weeks (see the Rich Task assignment).
