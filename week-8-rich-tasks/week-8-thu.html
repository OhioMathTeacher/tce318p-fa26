Week 8 · Thursday — Studio

Act II · test the task · does it make the thinking visible? · the first time your task meets a real solver
Today's goals: You brought a tentative task and three solutions. Today you find out whether it does its job — surfacing your learner's thinking on your question. You'll test it on a classmate, compare it against how real children solved it, pressure-test it with a genAI partner, then adapt.

1 · What your task has to do (~8 min)

  • The task is the heart of the lesson you're building — the During. Its whole job is to make your learner's thinking visible, so the work becomes evidence you can read against your question.
  • So today we test two things: does the task reveal the thinking your question is about? And is it posed so a child can enter it and show that thinking?

2 · Classmate test-drive — and let them struggle (~30 min)

  • Pair with someone not in your field placement. Take turns.
  • Poser: pose your task as you would to your learner, hand over the materials, then stop talking. Watch. Do not rescue, hint, or explain — even when they pause or head down a "wrong" path.
  • Solver: actually try it. Think aloud. Get stuck honestly — don't perform.
  • Debrief together: Did the work reveal thinking that bears on the poser's question? What in the posing was clear, and what was confusing? Where did the poser almost step in — and what happened when they held back? Name one change.

Purpose: the hardest part is not helping. Noticing how much you want to rescue — and holding back — is the exact move you'll need in the field.

3 · Compare with real children — together (~20 min)

  • Stay with your test-drive partner. Open the student solutions for your NRICH task (or the nearest one) and read them together — your partner is seeing them fresh, even if you already looked.
  • What strategies did real children use that neither of you did? What partial or surprising thinking shows up?
  • Set it beside what you predicted — and beside what your partner just did on the test-drive. Where were you right, and where did real children think differently?

Purpose: real children rarely think the way we predict. That gap — between your anticipation and their actual work — is what you're learning to read.

4 · Start this week's assignment — the Rich Task (~15 min + work time)

  • Look at the Rich Task assignment together — what it asks, how you'll write it up, and how it's assessed. Ask your questions now.
  • Then start working. As you revise your task, use TaskAssembler to get Alkimi's feedback — bundle your question, task, and your own three solutions (with photos of any drawn work) into one file, or copy the filled-in prompt to paste. Ask it to widen (what strategies did I miss?) and pressure-test (where could a child finish without showing the thinking I'm after? pitfalls? the posing?).
  • These are your solutions, not the real children's from step 3 — Alkimi critiques your design; reading real student thinking stays your work. It's a critic, not a ghostwriter: if it hands you a task, push back. Keep the trail of what you change and reject.

Purpose: the same move as your research question — AI widens the field; your judgment narrows it. You are the designer, and the rest of the task is yours to finish.

Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • Finish the Rich Task for this week — carry today's three rounds of feedback (the test-drive, the real children's solutions, Alkimi's push) into your revision, and record what you changed and what you rejected.
  • Next week you design the Before — the routine that opens your lesson and surfaces your learner's entry thinking on the same mathematics.
