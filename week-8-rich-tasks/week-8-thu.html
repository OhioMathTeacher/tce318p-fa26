Week 8 · Thursday — Studio

Act II · test the task · does it make the thinking visible? · the first time your task meets someone who has never seen it
Today's goals: You brought a task. Today you find out whether it does its job. People who have never seen it will solve it cold, you'll set their thinking beside how real children solved it, and only then will you take it to a genAI partner. Human first, machine second.

1 · What your task has to do (~8 min)

  • The task is the heart of the lesson you're building — the During. Its whole job is to make students' thinking visible, so the work becomes evidence you can read against your question.
  • So today we test two things: does the task reveal the thinking your question is about, and is it posed so a child can enter it and show that thinking?

2 · Cold test — your Field Prep Partner(s) solve it (~15 min)

  • You'll be matched with your Field Prep Partner(s) — a classmate, or a pair, who have not seen your task. That is the whole point: they meet it as a solver would.
  • You: pose your task as you would to students, hand over the materials, then stop talking. Watch. Do not rescue, hint, or explain — even when they pause or head down a "wrong" path. Photograph their work — you'll need it.
  • Your partner(s): actually try it. Think aloud. Get stuck honestly — don't perform.

Purpose: the hardest part is not helping. Noticing how badly you want to rescue — and holding back — is the exact move you'll need in the field.

3 · Compare and judge the task — together (~15 min)

  • Now look together at two things: how your partner(s) just solved it, and the published NRICH solutions for your task. Your partner(s) are seeing the NRICH solutions fresh.
  • Pull out three genuinely different solution strategies for your task from what you have between you — the live solve(s) and the NRICH solutions. Where did real solvers think in ways you didn't expect?
  • Then ask your partner(s) straight: is this task genuinely rich, and does it fit your research question? Capture their feedback.

Purpose: real solvers rarely think the way we predict. That gap — and whether the task is open enough to hold three real strategies — is what tells you it can carry evidence.

4 · Then Alkimi — and start the assignment (~30–40 min)

  • Now, and only now, take your task to Alkimi — give it your task, grade level, and research question with the prompt from the assignment, and follow up on what it says.
  • Alkimi is a critic, not a designer, and the strategies it names are possibilities to watch for — not how children actually think. The real children's thinking stayed your work in block 3.
  • Start the Rich Task assignment: revise your task from both reads, and begin the two-reads reflection. Read the assignment together first — ask your questions now.

Purpose: the same move as your research question — the human read comes first and grounds you; the machine widens the field after. Your judgment narrows it.

Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • Finish the Rich Task assignment for this week — carry both reads (your partners' cold solve and the NRICH comparison, then Alkimi's check) into your revision, and record what you changed and what you rejected.
  • Next week you design the Before — the routine that opens your lesson and surfaces students' entry thinking on the same mathematics.
