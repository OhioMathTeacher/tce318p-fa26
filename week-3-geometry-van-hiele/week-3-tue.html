Week 3 · Tuesday — Studio

Geometry, 2.G / 3.G attributes · Poser · the same figure, seen different ways
Today's goals: Run What Do You See? / How Do You Know? geometry talks as both poser and learner. Feel how one figure gets seen many ways — "diamond," "rhombus," "parallelogram" — and that the spread isn't error, it's real. We name what it is on Thursday; today you just live it.

1 · Warm-up + set the stance (~10 min)

  • One geometry talk together: flash a figure, then "What do you see?" — collect a few — then "How do you know?"
  • Name the only move today: say what you see, then how you know. As poser you ask, never tell — you're gathering how someone sees, not correcting it.

Purpose: What do you see? cues nothing — a first grader says "a door," someone else says "a special rectangle," and both are honest. That openness is what makes the talk a probe.

2 · Run the bank in parallel groups (~50 min)

  • Three routines over one shape library — each a structured What do you see?:
  • WODB Which One Doesn't Belong? — four figures; pick one that doesn't belong and defend it (every figure can be defended).
  • Same & Different — two or three figures; say what's the same and what's different.
  • Sort — a handful of figures; group them and state your rule.
  • Each group facilitates 1–2 talks, then the whole class compares. Everyone gets reps posing and meets the range of ways a figure gets seen.

Purpose: three routines keep the demand varied, but all of them surface the same thing — how differently people classify the same form.

If you finish early

  • Take one figure your group already ran and find a reason to include it that no one gave. How many honestly-different "sees" can one figure hold?
Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • Collect the noticings without naming them: we described the same shapes in strikingly different ways, each with a reason. Hold that — Thursday it gets a name.
  • Homework, in order: (1) open nVoke and run the van Hiele Geometry pack in Diagnostic mode — type what you see for each card, then Export the PDF or keep it on your device; (2) read van Hiele (1999), "Developing Geometric Thinking through Activities That Begin with Play" (link in Canvas → JSTOR); (3) on an index card, answer: how might the routines we ran (WODB / Same & Different / Sort) be used together with van Hiele levels? Bring the card and your results Thursday.
  • Thursday: we open nVoke together, put a name to the spread — van Hiele levels — and learn to hear the level in a learner's reasoning.
