Week 3 · Thursday — Clinic

Geometry, 2.G / 3.G attributes · Poser · listening for the level
Today's goals: Put a name to Tuesday's spread — van Hiele levels — and learn to hear the level in how a learner reasons, not just what they name. Then build a routine designed to reveal it. The aim is to locate, not to rank: you diagnose in order to offer the right next experience.

1 · Open nVoke together — the van Hiele pack, live (~20 min)

  • As a class, open nVoke and run the six-card van Hiele Geometry pack in Diagnostic mode — the same one you ran for homework — and build one shared report together. Bring your own results up alongside it.
  • We field every question about the app now, so it's second nature when you take it home.
  • Watch what the shared report shows: the same six cards, described many different ways. That variation is today's raw material.

Purpose: redoing the homework together makes the whole class one dataset — the spread you'll learn to read is right there on the screen, in your own words.

2 · Reverse-engineer one card (~25 min)

  • Each group takes one of the six cards and works out: how is it linked to van Hiele? what is it engineered to tease out? which levels does it split — and how would you hear the difference?
  • Anchor: the Level 0 eye names by appearance ("a diamond," "a symbol"); the Level 1 eye reasons from properties ("equal sides," "straight sides, closed"); the Level 2 eye relates them ("a square is a special rectangle").
  • Short share-out: each group names its card's fault line — the reason two learners give the same answer for opposite reasons.

Purpose: a card only "works" if you can say what it's probing — reverse-engineering one is how you learn to design one, and the diagnosis lives in the How do you know?, never the label alone.

3 · Author a fault-line routine (~20 min)

  • In nVoke's Builder, each group composes its own WODB / Same & Different / Sort designed to split levels — a routine where the answer alone won't tell you who's where, but the reasoning will.
  • Export your routine so every group member carries the same copy home.

Purpose: you can't build a routine that separates levels unless you can already hear them — building is the proof you understood the ladder.

4 · Question closer (QFT) (~12 min · in pairs)

Your weekly Question Formulation — four rules: ask as many as you can · don't stop to answer or judge · write each exactly as stated · turn any statement into a question.

  • Q-focus: "You should meet a learner where they are." (van Hiele)
  • Brainstorm 10+ questions off it, prioritize your top 3, and deposit one into the class question bank.

Turn a provocation into questions worth exploring. Not collected — thinking practice; your one question joins the bank that seeds the inquiry work ahead.

If you finish early

  • Trade routines with another group and run theirs cold: what level do you think it's built to split? Then check your read against theirs.
Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • Same act as the last two weeks — reading a learner — now aimed at where they are developmentally. The pick tells you little; the justification is the whole diagnosis.
  • Take-home: Diagnostic Study — finish your routine, pose it to a friend, family member, or a student not in our section, collect their responses in nVoke, then locate their level with evidence and name the next experience you'd offer (see Canvas).
  • Next week: step from Poser to Planner — anticipating how a discussion will go before you run it (measurement & data).
