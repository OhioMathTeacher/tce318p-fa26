Week 10 · Tuesday — Studio

Act II · design the After + assemble the lesson · how will you capture what students' thinking leaves behind?
Today's goals: Design the After — the piece that captures students' thinking so you can analyze it later — and assemble your Before, During, and After into one Investigation Plan. You leave with a complete plan, ready for a colleague to pressure-test on Thursday.

1 · Design your After — the capture (~25 min)

  • Your During is where the thinking happens — but thinking evaporates. The After is how you capture it: a short artifact you physically collect, plus a question or two that pushes the reasoning. Skip it and you finish the lesson holding nothing to analyze.
  • Afters come in a few forms — a quick look, so you can pick what fits what you need to see:
    • Written — an exit slip, a "jot how you know" prompt, or a stem: "I know ___ because ___."
    • Drawn — "draw where 3/4 would sit and label it," or show it a second way.
    • Photographed — a photo of a student's work, or of the board after a share.
    • Spoken, captured — a quick "convince me" you record in a note.
  • Weak vs. strong, same task: "did everyone get it? thumbs up" leaves you a nodding room and nothing to read; "draw where 3/4 sits and write one sentence on how you decided" leaves you holding the reasoning. The test: would a student's response leave me holding evidence I could hold up against my research question? If it can be answered without revealing any thinking, it isn't a capture yet.
  • Draft yours now. Write the After for your task — the artifact you'll collect and the question(s) that push the thinking — then trade with a neighbor: could a student finish theirs without showing any thinking? You'll bring it straight into assembly next.

Purpose: the After is the most-skipped, least-planned piece — and the one that decides whether you walk out with data. You draft it here so you have all three pieces to assemble, and so Thursday's reviewer has a whole plan to test.

2 · Assemble the whole instrument (~30 min)

  • Open the Investigation Plan template (the 3-pager) and lay in all three pieces: your Before (the Week 9 routine), your During (the Week 8 rich task), and the After you just drafted. Fill the connective tissue the template asks for — the mathematical goal, standards, anticipated responses, and timing.
  • Now read it as one lesson and check the seams: does it read as a single lesson on one concept, or three activities stapled together? Does the Before flow into the During without handing over the move (the give-away you guarded last week)? Does the After capture what the During surfaced — or ask about something the task never raised?
  • The lesson is an instrument: every section should answer "what will this let me see about how students understand this concept?" If a section doesn't, it's decoration — cut or sharpen it.

Purpose: three strong pieces do not make a lesson if they don't nest. Assembly is where you find the gaps between them — and where the arc either points at your question or wanders off it.

3 · The assignment and the field ahead (~20 min)

  • What you submit. Open the Investigation Plan assignment together: your revised plan (Document 1) and a short note on how you used your two reads (Document 2), scored on the rubric. Ask your questions about the deliverable now.
  • What you do with it. In Weeks 11–12 you take this instrument into your placement and run it. Read the field-teaching guidelines together: you teach one or more of your pieces (Before, During, and/or After) — not necessarily the whole lesson — with one or more students, and you collect the work. Pseudonyms; a backup student on the same concept if yours is out.
  • Quick go-around: given what you know of your placement, which piece(s) are you likely to teach, and what will you be able to collect?

Purpose: the design grade is settled by the plan, not by what the placement allows — but knowing the field constraints now lets you build a lesson you can actually run and collect from.

Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • Before Thursday, finish assembling your whole lesson in the template — Before, During, and After — with your research question written at the top where a reader can see it.
  • Thursday is a working session: a colleague reads your whole plan and pressure-tests it against your question — would a student's response actually answer it? — then you take it to a genAI partner. You revise from both reads before the field (see the Investigation Plan assignment).
