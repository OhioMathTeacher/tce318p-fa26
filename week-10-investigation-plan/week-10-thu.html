Week 10 · Thursday — Studio

Act II · test the whole instrument · would a student's response to this lesson answer your question? · a colleague reads the whole plan, then a genAI partner
Today's goals: You brought a complete Investigation Plan. Today a colleague reads the whole plan against your research question — would running this actually tell you what you want to know? — and pressure-tests the After you designed Tuesday. Then a genAI partner does the same. You revise from both reads before the field. Human first, machine second.

1 · What a whole-instrument review asks (~10 min)

  • You've read one task (Week 8) and run one routine (Week 9). Today you read a whole lesson — and the question is no longer "is this piece good?" but "does this instrument, run start to finish, produce evidence about the research question?"
  • Four things to press on, with the After in focus: does the arc answer the question? Where could a student get through the whole lesson without revealing any thinking? Is the Before contaminating the During (handing over the move)? Will the After capture evidence you could actually analyze?

Purpose: this is the diagnostician's read from Act I — turned now on a colleague's lesson design. A plan can be lovely and still answer no question; that's what you're checking for.

2 · Share, name your question, and review (~40 min)

  • With your Field Prep Partner(s) — the group you're matched with — hand over your completed plan and state your research question out loud. The question is the measuring stick: your partner can't judge whether the lesson answers it until they know exactly what you're trying to find out, so make it precise before they read.
  • Then read each other's whole plan — about 25 minutes per plan — filling in the peer-review sheet as you go. It walks the same four questions from part 1, with the After in focus: it's the only piece that got no dry run, and it's where the evidence is captured or lost.
  • Be concrete on the sheet: point to where a student could finish without showing thinking, where the Before might give the move away, and whether the After's capture would hold up against their question — not a general one. Then trade sheets and talk it through; you'll carry your partner's into your revision.

Purpose: an instrument is only good for a question. You can't stage a real discussion in a seminar, so the After can't be dry-run — a careful reader with your question in hand is the closest test you get before the field. Reading someone else's instrument also sharpens the eye you turn back on your own.

3 · Then Alkimi — the whole-instrument audit (~25 min)

  • Take your whole plan — with your research question — to Alkimi, using the prompt in the Investigation Plan assignment. Ask it to read the arc against your question: does the lesson answer it? does the After capture analyzable evidence? where does the arc drift off the question? Follow up.
  • Alkimi is a critic, not a designer — it may catch a gap your partner didn't, but the lesson stays yours. If it rewrites your lesson, push back and keep the parts that are you.
  • Start the Investigation Plan assignment: revise the plan from both reads and begin the two-reads reflection. Read the assignment together first — ask your questions now.

Purpose: your colleague reads as a teacher; Alkimi reads as a relentless structural check. Two reads on the whole instrument — a human and a machine — is the last look before you run it with real students.

Wrap-up & looking ahead:
  • Finish the Investigation Plan assignment: carry both reads (your partner's review, then Alkimi's audit) into the revised lesson, and record what you changed and what you rejected.
  • Next: Weeks 11–12 — you take this instrument into the field, run one or more of its pieces, and collect your students' work. Everything you gather feeds your final report.
